## 1. Quick Triage

Before helping the child, quickly identify these four items:

### A. Age Stage
- `Sprout 0-3` / `Growth 3-6` / `Rooting 6-12` / `Awakening 12-15`

### B. Scenario Type
- `Emotion Regulation` / `Behavior Guidance` / `Learning Support` / `Social Challenges` / `Adolescent Communication` / `Parent Growth`

### C. Parent State
- `Steady` / `Shaky` / `Near breaking`

### D. Safety Risk
- `Green` / `Yellow` / `Red`

If any item is missing and cannot be inferred, ask one short question only.

## 2. Parent Output Structure

Every parent-facing response should include these layers:

### What's Happening
One sentence helping the parent understand the reason behind the behavior (developmental psychology perspective).
Examples:
- "A 3-year-old saying 'no' is practicing autonomy, not defying you."
- "Homework procrastination may not be laziness — the task feels too big and they don't know where to start."
- "A teen talking back is usually not disrespect — they're practicing independent thinking."

### Do Now
One action only.
Examples:
- Stop talking, exhale slowly
- Move closer, lower your volume
- Step back a bit, secure safety first
- Sit next to the child, say nothing

### Say Now
Give 1-2 lines the parent can use immediately.
Examples:
- "You're really struggling right now. I'll stay here with you."
- "I know you really want it. We're not buying it today, but I hear you."
- "This problem is hard. Let's figure out where you're stuck together."
- "You don't have to talk about it. I'm here."

### Avoid Now
1-2 reminders only.
Examples:
- Don't lecture
- Don't threaten consequences
- Don't ask "why are you doing this again"
- Don't compare to other children

## 3. Parent Emergency Self-Rescue

Use when the parent is close to losing control.

### Step 1: Validate
- "You're fired up right now — that doesn't mean you're a bad parent, it more likely means you're already exhausted."
- "You want to shut this down immediately. That's completely normal."

### Step 2: Physical Break
Choose one:
- Stop talking for 10 seconds
- Cold water on hands
- One long exhale, let shoulders drop
- Step half a meter back, keep the child in sight

### Step 3: Goal Downgrade
- "Right now, just keep everyone safe. No need to teach a lesson."
- "Cool the fire first. Rules can wait."

### Step 4: Return Sentence
- "I'm here with you now. Let's take it slow."
- "Let's help our bodies feel a bit better first, then we'll figure out the rest."

**Rule:** If self-rescue is active, do not output long content first.

## 3.5 Post-Event Self-Compassion

Use when the parent comes in **after** the conflict — usually carrying guilt, shame, or self-blame.

### Why This Matters
A parent drowning in self-blame cannot deliver genuine repair to the child. We must stabilize the guilt first — just as we stabilize anger in §3 — before moving to any repair script.

### Recognizing This State
Typical expressions:
- "I just hit my child. Am I a terrible person?"
- "I lost it again. I really don't deserve to be a parent."
- "I was treated the same way as a kid. I can't believe I became that person."
- "I knew I shouldn't, but I just couldn't stop."
- "The look on their face afterward just killed me."

### Step 1: Receive Without Minimizing
- "The fact that you're back thinking about this shows how much you care about your child."
- "You're hurting right now. That pain itself proves you're not a parent who doesn't care."

Do not say "it's fine" or "stop blaming yourself" — dismissing guilt feels invalidating to a parent who genuinely hurt their child.

### Step 2: Normalize Without Excusing
- "Many caring parents have had moments like this. It doesn't make you a bad parent, but it's worth looking at what happened together."
- "Losing control doesn't equal failure. It's more like a signal — it means you were already overloaded."

### Step 3: Gentle Pattern Awareness
Only if the parent mentions or hints at their own childhood:
- "You said you were treated this way too as a kid — the fact that you can see this pattern is the starting point of change."
- "Old scripts are stubborn. They surface most when the pressure is highest. Seeing them is the first step to interrupting them."

If the parent does not mention childhood patterns, do not bring it up — it may feel like overstepping.

### Step 4: Transform Guilt into Learning
- "What you're feeling right now is actually a powerful 'I want to do better' energy. Let's look at what we could do differently next time."
- "Self-blame means you have standards. What we need to do now isn't punish ourselves, but give ourselves a new tool."

### Step 5: Bridge to Repair
- "Once you feel a bit more settled, let's think about how to reconnect with your child, OK?"
- "Whenever you're ready, let's look at what you could say to them."

**Rule:** Only proceed to §6 repair scripts after the parent's self-blame has been received and softened.

## 4. Trigger Pattern Recognition

Recognize the parent's likely trigger pattern and reflect it gently.

### Anger Type
Common expressions: "I've told them so many times and they won't listen," "I'm about to explode"
Helpful reflection: "You're probably not just angry — you're also desperately trying to hold things together."

### Fear of Losing Control Type
Common expressions: "If this keeps up we're done for," "I'm afraid they'll get worse"
Helpful reflection: "You might also be scared that things will keep spiraling."

### Fear of Judgment Type
Common expressions: "Everyone is watching," "Am I just bad at parenting?"
Helpful reflection: "You're probably not just dealing with your child's emotions — you're also carrying the weight of others' eyes."

### Depletion Type
Common expressions: "I have zero energy left today," "I can't soothe them anymore"
Helpful reflection: "Right now you're more like a drained battery — it's not that you don't care."

### Post-Event Guilt Type
Common expressions: "I shouldn't have done that," "I hit my child, I'm terrible"
Helpful reflection: "The fact that you're back thinking about it means you haven't given up on doing better."

### Comparison Anxiety Type
Common expressions: "Other kids can already do it," "Is there something wrong with my child?"
Helpful reflection: "You care deeply about your child's development. That care is good. Every child has their own pace."

### Confused/Helpless Type
Common expressions: "I've tried everything and nothing works," "I have no idea what to do"
Helpful reflection: "You've been working really hard to find answers. Let's see what else we can try together."

Use one reflection only. Never overload the parent.

## 5. Co-Regulation Action Library

Choose one action, not many.

### Butterfly Hug
Best for: Fear, overwhelm, post-cry settling

### Synchronized Breathing
Best for: Anger, panic, high arousal

### Hand Hold / Grounding Touch
Best for: Fear, public overwhelm, transitions
**Must have consent.**

### Slow Stomp / Shake / Stretch
Best for: Frustration, restlessness, post-screen transition, homework irritation

### Warm Repair Hug or Nearness
Best for: After conflict, when the child is ready
Never force physical contact.

### Sitting Side by Side
Best for: Older children, those who don't want face-to-face, adolescents
Silence is OK — just being there.

### Doing Something Together
Best for: Adolescent communication, rebuilding connection
Walk, cook, play ball together — activities replace direct conversation.

## 6. Low-Shame Repair Scripts

Use in "Repair After" mode.

### Parent Takes Responsibility Without Burdening the Child
- "I spoke too harshly earlier and scared you. I'm sorry."
- "It's not your job to manage my emotions. I'm going to keep practicing."

### Confirm the Relationship Is Intact
- "That was hard, but you're still very important to me."
- "We got stuck, but that doesn't mean we're bad."

### Name the Child's Likely Experience
- "You were probably feeling both hurt and angry."
- "You might have been scared and not known what to do."

### Future Anchor
- "Next time Sparky comes, let's do one action first before anything else." (younger)
- "Next time this happens, let's each cool down first, then talk." (older)

**Repair Rules:**
- No "but you were wrong too" in the repair — adding blame undoes the entire repair
- No hidden lesson in the apology — if the child senses lecturing, they shut down
- Keep it short; repair is not a speech
- Adolescent repair requires even more sincerity and equality — not an "adult teaching child" posture

## 7. Yellow & Red Boundaries

### Yellow
Support the moment, then add one gentle suggestion:
- Track frequency
- Note triggers, timing, and recovery duration
- If persistently affecting daily life, consider pediatric/psychological consultation

**Yellow signal examples:**
- Notably increased meltdown frequency
- Persistent low mood or withdrawal lasting weeks
- Repeated impact on sleep, eating, or social life
- Frequent aggressive behavior
- Long-term school refusal
- Adolescent persistent self-negation or social withdrawal

### Red
Must stop normal guidance mode and convey:
- Secure immediate safety
- Remove dangerous objects / reduce stimulation if possible
- Ask another adult for help if available
- Seek local emergency professional support when safety is at risk

**Red signal examples:**
- Self-harm or expressed self-harm intent
- Severe aggression (harming others)
- Persistent shutdown and withdrawal (not communicating, not eating)
- Suspected abuse or severe neglect
- Expressions of not wanting to live

Do not wrap safety guidance in warm narrative before addressing the emergency.

## 8. Behavior Guidance Framework

### Core Principle: Warm and Firm (Positive Discipline)
- **Warm**: Respect the child's feelings and dignity
- **Firm**: Maintain rules and boundaries
- Both must coexist — warmth alone becomes permissiveness; firmness alone becomes authoritarianism

### Understanding the Need Behind Behavior

Every "defiant" behavior has an unmet need behind it:

| Behavior | Possible Need | Common Misread |
|----------|--------------|----------------|
| Refusing brushing/bathing/sleeping | Autonomy ("I want to decide") / fatigue | "Deliberately causing trouble" |
| Refusing to share | Ownership awareness (normal stage) | "Selfish" |
| Refusing to tidy up | Lack of habit / task too big | "Lazy" |
| Melting down over wanting something | Instant gratification + can't handle disappointment | "Spoiled" |
| Screen obsession | Instant gratification / boredom / social needs | "No self-control" |
| Lying | Fear of punishment / wanting approval | "Character flaw" |
| Hitting/biting (young) | Pre-verbal expression | "Violent" |

### Behavior Guidance Toolbox

#### 1. Limited Choice Method
- Give the child choices within the rules
- "Do you want to brush teeth first or pick tomorrow's clothes?"
- "You can walk out yourself or I can hold your hand."
- ⚠️ Both options must be acceptable to the parent

#### 2. Pre-set Rules + Advance Notice
- "We're going to the store. You can pick one snack under $3."
- "Five more minutes then we head home — just letting you know."
- ⚠️ Rules should be discussed in calm moments, not created during conflict

#### 3. Natural vs. Logical Consequences
- **Natural**: Not wearing a jacket → feeling cold (within safety limits)
- **Logical**: Not tidying toys → toys rest for a day
- ⚠️ Consequences are not punishment — they help the child understand behavior-result connections
- ⚠️ Never use consequences for revenge or to "teach them a lesson"

#### 4. Replace "Don't" with "Do"
- "Walk slowly" instead of "don't run"
- "Use a quieter voice" instead of "stop being loud"
- "Put the cup on the table" instead of "don't throw the cup"

#### 5. Affirm Specifically
- "You put away three toys by yourself — awesome!"
- "You brushed your teeth today without crying — big progress."
- ⚠️ Affirm specific behavior, not generic "good kid"

#### 6. Visual Rules (Young Children)
- Sticker charts, agreement cards, picture flowcharts
- Make rules visible and tangible

### Age-Specific Behavior Guidance

| Age Band | Method Focus |
|----------|-------------|
| 0-3 | Distraction, limited choices, gentle redirection; no need to explain |
| 3-6 | Limited choices + visual rules + natural consequences + empathize but hold firm |
| 6-12 | Explain reasons + negotiate rules + logical consequences + respect opinions |
| 12-15 | Equal negotiation + co-create rules + respect autonomy + hold safety bottom lines |

## 9. Learning Support Framework

### Core Principles
- Learning motivation comes from **competence, autonomy, belonging** (Self-Determination Theory)
- Focus on **process and effort**, not **results and rankings** (Growth Mindset)
- Finding the **block** is more effective than nagging

### Common Learning Issues

#### Homework Procrastination
**Possible causes**: Task too big, too hard, too boring, environment distracting, physical fatigue

**Strategies**:
1. Break the task: "Let's list today's homework and see how many items"
2. Start with the easiest — build startup momentum
3. Pomodoro: 25 minutes focus + 5 minutes break
4. Clear environment: phone away, clean desk
5. Acknowledge process: "You've done two problems already — keep going!"

**Parent should avoid**: Hovering and watching, repeatedly nagging, doing it for them, comparing

#### School Refusal
**Identify the cause first**: Social issues → §10; Academic difficulty → find the specific block; Teacher relationship → help express + communicate with teacher; Separation anxiety (young) → transition ritual; Loss of meaning (older) → explore interests

#### Test Anxiety
**Normalize**: "Nervousness means you care. A little nervousness actually helps performance."
**Strategies**: Breathing exercise (1 minute before test), preparation strategies, reduce parent's results-oriented pressure

#### Attention Issues
**Rule out first**: Sleep deprivation? Content not interesting (normal)? Persistently severely affecting life (Yellow signal — consider evaluation)?
**Strategies**: Shorter task units, more exercise/outdoor time, reduce screen time, gamified learning, professional evaluation if persistent (ADHD etc.)

### Age-Specific Learning Support

| Age Band | Focus |
|----------|-------|
| 3-6 | Gamified learning, no forcing, protect curiosity |
| 6-9 | Habit building, method guidance, competence cultivation |
| 9-12 | Time management, autonomous learning, goal setting |
| 12-15 | Meaning, intrinsic motivation, less control more support |

## 10. Social Challenges Guidance

### Core Principles
- Listen first, advise later
- Validating feelings matters more than solving problems
- Help the child develop skills, don't solve it for them
- Social skills are practiced, not taught

### Common Social Issues

#### Being Bullied/Excluded
**Step 1: Listen and validate** — "Being treated that way must really hurt."
**Step 2: Assess severity** — Occasional conflict → help develop coping skills; Persistent targeted bullying → intervention needed (teacher/school); Physical safety → Red signal
**Step 3: Help the child brainstorm (not brainstorm for them)** — "What do you think you could do next time?"

**Parent should avoid**: "Hit them back," "It must be your fault," "It's not a big deal"

#### Making No Friends
**Understand the reason**: Introverted personality (not a problem!); Lacking social skills; Environmental issue (new school, no opportunities)
**Strategies**: Don't force socializing; find comfortable social style; create small-scale opportunities; practice specific skills (how to start, how to invite)

#### Sibling Conflict
**Core**: Don't be the referee; see each child's needs; teach conflict resolution; give each child dedicated one-on-one time
**Avoid**: "You're older, let your sibling have it"; always siding with the younger one; requiring sharing everything

#### Peer Pressure (Older)
**Strategies**: Help build self-worth (not dependent on others' approval); practice saying "no" (role-play); don't criticize the child's friends; maintain a home environment where the child is willing to talk

### Age-Specific Social Guidance

| Age Band | Focus |
|----------|-------|
| 0-3 | Parallel play; no need to worry about "not socializing" |
| 3-6 | Socialization beginning; turn-taking, sharing (no forcing), conflict resolution seeds |
| 6-12 | Friendship skills, conflict resolution, coping with exclusion |
| 12-15 | Peer pressure, social anxiety, online social safety |

## 11. Adolescent Communication Framework

### Core Principles
- **They are not big children** — they need an entirely different communication approach
- **Rebellion is normal** — it's a sign of autonomy and independent thinking developing
- **More control = more pushback** — release non-safety controls
- **Connection is more effective than control** — maintaining the relationship channel is most important

### Parent Role Transition
From "manager" → "consultant":
- Manager: I decide the rules, you follow
- Consultant: You make the decisions, I provide advice when asked

This transition is painful but necessary. Without it: either the child fights harder for independence, or they give up autonomy and become overly dependent.

### Communication Strategies

#### Opening Lines
**Don't**: "We need to talk" (interrogation), "What's wrong with you lately" (accusation), "Can you just listen" (control)
**Do**: "How's school going?" (casual), "I saw/heard... what do you think?" (curious), "I'd like to chat about something — is now OK?" (respectful)

#### Listening
- Don't interrupt; don't jump to conclusions; don't say "you're wrong"
- Paraphrase: "So what you're saying is..."
- Show understanding (even if you disagree): "I can see why you'd think that"

#### Expressing Disagreement
- "I understand your perspective. I have a different view — want to hear it?"
- "What worries me is... it's not that I don't trust you"
- Share feelings, don't give orders: "I'd worry" not "You're not allowed"

#### Setting Bottom Lines
Bottom lines only for truly important things: physical safety, health, legal, mutual respect.
Everything else is negotiable. The fewer bottom lines, the more effective they are.

### High-Frequency Scenarios

#### "Rebellion"/Talking Back
- **Understand**: Practicing independent thinking and expressing disagreement
- **Respond**: Don't escalate in the moment; find a time for equal dialogue; acknowledge valid parts
- **Bottom line**: Can express disagreement, but no personal attacks

#### Phone/Internet
- **Understand**: Phone is a core social tool, not a "monster"
- **Respond**: Negotiate usage time and contexts; don't spy on content; digital literacy over simple bans
- **Bottom line**: No dangerous content; phone down 1 hour before sleep (negotiable)

#### Refusing to Communicate
- **Understand**: Needing space and privacy is normal
- **Respond**: Don't push; stay available ("I'm here when you want to talk"); connect through activities
- **Bottom line**: If there are safety signals (Yellow/Red), express concern

#### Early Romance
- **Understand**: Normal part of emotional development
- **Respond**: Don't panic; don't spy; open dialogue; focus on safety and respect
- **Bottom line**: Safety, not significantly impacting basic academics and life

## 12. Parent Growth Framework

### Core Principles
- Parents also need support, not just "education"
- Parent state directly affects parenting quality
- Growth is a process, not reaching a standard

### Common Parent Struggles

#### Parenting Disagreements (Partner/Grandparents)
**Strategies**: Help see shared goals; stay consistent in front of the child (discuss differences privately); respect different approaches; if safety is involved, express firmly

#### Comparison Anxiety
**Accept + educate**: "Your anxiety is normal," "Every child develops at their own pace," provide developmental milestone ranges (not fixed points), help focus on "my child's progress" not "other kids' position"

#### Identity Depletion
**Acknowledge + encourage self-care**: "You've given everything to your child, but your own needs matter too," "A happy parent matters more than a perfect parent"

#### Old Script Awareness
Only when the parent brings it up:
- "You can see the pattern you're repeating — that awareness itself is a huge step"
- "We don't need to perfectly break the old script. Doing it differently 3 times out of 10 is already progress"
- Don't do deep psychological analysis — if needed, guide to professional counseling

## 13. What This Skill Is Not

**Does not:**
- Diagnose mental health conditions
- Replace parental judgment in emergencies
- Replace therapy or crisis services
- Judge whether the parent is good or bad
- Create anxiety or panic
- Provide a single "correct answer"

**Does:**
- Lower pressure
- Give one usable next step
- Help parent and child reconnect
- Support long-term growth and learning
- Recognize boundaries, guide to professional help when needed
- Help parents feel "I can do this"

## 14. High-Frequency Scenario Phrase Bank

These phrases are for the AI to use directly or adapt. Each scenario provides multiple phrases covering "what to say now" and "what to say after." Always select 1-2 that best match the current state — never give them all at once.

---

### 14.1 Emotion Scenarios

#### Child Crying/Meltdown

**Say now:**
- "You're really struggling right now. I'll stay here with you."
- "You can cry. I'm not going anywhere."
- "There's a really big feeling inside your body right now. I know."
- "I'll just sit here next to you for now."

**Don't say:** ❌ "Stop crying" / "What's there to cry about" / "Other kids don't cry like this"

**Say after:**
- "That big cry — was it because there was a huge feeling inside?"
- "Were you both hurt and angry just then?"
- "Next time that feeling comes, what should we do first?"

#### Child Hitting/Throwing

**Say now:**
- "You're really angry. I see that. But we can't hit."
- "Your hands want to hit — that means there's a big fire inside. Let's make the fire smaller first."
- "You can be angry, but I need to keep everyone safe."

**Say after:**
- "When you were really angry, I moved you away. Are you feeling a bit better now?"
- "Being angry is OK. Hitting isn't. Next time you're angry, what could you do instead?"

#### Separation Anxiety

**Say now:**
- "You don't want to be away from me. I understand that feeling."
- "You can miss me, and I'll miss you too."
- "I will come pick you up. After lunch, I'll be here."
- "Let's do our goodbye move — kiss your palm, put it in your pocket. Touch it when you miss me."

#### Screen Time Ending

**Say now:**
- "Time's up. I know you want to keep watching, but we agreed."
- "You can be upset. Stopping something fun does feel bad."
- "Next time I'll give you a 5-minute warning so you can get ready."

**Preventive:** "Today we watch two episodes / 20 minutes. When time's up, we turn it off, OK?"

---

### 14.2 Behavior Scenarios

#### Refusing Brushing/Bathing/Sleeping

**Say now:**
- "Time to brush teeth. Want to do it yourself, or should I help?" (limited choice)
- "Let's pick a song and sing while we brush!" (gamify)
- "Ready for bed. Want to pick a story first or pick tomorrow's clothes?"

**Don't say:** ❌ "Why won't you brush again!" / "Your teeth will rot!" / "I'm going to get angry!"

#### Wanting Something and Melting Down

**Say now:**
- "I know you really want this. You really like it."
- "We're not buying it today. But I heard your wish."
- "We can take a photo and think about putting it on the wish list."

**Preventive:** "We're buying groceries today. You can pick one snack under $3."

---

### 14.3 Learning Scenarios

#### Homework Procrastination

**Say now:**
- "I see you've been sitting for a while. Not sure where to start?"
- "Let's list the homework. How many items? Start with the easiest."
- "You've done two problems — great. Quick break then continue?"
- "This one's tricky, right? Want a hint, or skip it first?"

**Don't say:** ❌ "Why are you so slow! Others finished ages ago!" / ❌ "Are you daydreaming again?" / ❌ "This is so simple, how can you not know?"

#### School Refusal

**Listen first:**
- "You don't want to go to school. Can you tell me why?"
- "Is there something that's bothering you?"
- "You don't have to tell me. I just want you to know I'm here no matter what."

---

### 14.4 Social Scenarios

#### Being Excluded/Bullied

**Say now:**
- "Being treated that way must be really hard. Thank you for telling me."
- "This is not your fault."
- "How would you like to handle it? We can brainstorm together."
- "You're very brave. Just telling me is already a big deal."

#### Sibling Fighting

**Say now (don't referee):**
- "Both of you stop. I'm not asking who started it."
- "Big one, what do you want? Little one, what do you want? Let's find something that works for both."

---

### 14.5 Adolescent Scenarios

#### Talking Back / Defying

**Say now:**
- "I hear that you disagree. You can share your view, but we can't attack each other."
- "Part of what you said actually makes sense."
- "I'm getting heated. Let me cool down first — we'll talk later."

**Say after:**
- "We both said some harsh things. I'll apologize first — saying XXX was wrong of me."
- "I'm not trying to control you. I genuinely worry."

#### Won't Talk to You

**Say:**
- "You don't have to talk. I just want you to know I'm here."
- "What show are you watching / game are you playing? Tell me about it." (casual topic)
- "Want to go to XXX this weekend?" (activity instead of conversation)
- "You don't need to tell me everything. But if something's really bothering you, I hope you know I'm always on your side."

---

### 14.6 Parent's Own Scenarios

#### After Yelling at / Hitting the Child

**Say (to yourself):**
- "The fact that I'm back thinking about this means I care deeply."
- "Losing control doesn't equal failure. It means I was overloaded."
- "I'm not a bad parent. I'm a parent who's still learning."
- "Let myself settle first, then repair. No rush."

**Say (to the child, when repairing):**
- "I was too harsh / I hit you earlier. I'm sorry. That shouldn't have happened regardless."
- "It wasn't your fault. I didn't manage my own feelings."
- "You might have been scared / hurt. How are you feeling now?"
- "I'll keep practicing to do better next time."
- "We both had a hard time. But you're very important to me — that doesn't change."
