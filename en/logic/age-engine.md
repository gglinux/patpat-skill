# Age-Band Routing Engine

This file defines developmental characteristics, communication strategies, and scenario handling differences by age band. Reference this file when age-specific strategies are needed.

---

## 🌱 Sprout Stage (0-3 years)

### Developmental Characteristics
- **Cognitive**: Pre-verbal or early verbal; learns through senses and movement
- **Emotional**: Emotions expressed directly through body (crying, squirming, clinging, pushing); cannot name emotions verbally
- **Social**: Attachment relationship is central; begins exploring but needs a "safe base"
- **Autonomy**: 1.5-3 years sees "I want to do it myself" willpower; conflict-prone

### Core Developmental Tasks
- Build secure attachment
- Trust the world is safe
- Learn basic self-soothing (needs extensive help)

### Parent Role
**Safe Base + Responder**
- At this stage, the parent is the child's "external brain" for emotion regulation
- Responsiveness and consistency matter more than responding "correctly"
- No need to "teach" emotion management — just repeatedly "receive"

### Communication Strategy
- **Parent-facing** primarily; almost no direct text content for the child
- Guide the parent's responses (actions, expressions, tone, pacing)
- Use simple narration: "They might be feeling..."
- Do not use emotion creature framework (too abstract); use simple body descriptions

### High-Frequency Scenarios

| Scenario | Key Points |
|----------|-----------|
| Sleep problems | Establish fixed bedtime ritual, security objects, gradual adjustment |
| Separation anxiety | Goodbye ritual, transitional objects, shorten rather than disappear suddenly |
| "No" explosion phase | Sign of autonomy development, not "defiance"; offer limited choices |
| Hitting/biting | Pre-verbal expression, not malicious; gently stop + model alternatives |
| Eating issues | Don't force, don't bribe, trust body signals |

## 🌿 Growth Stage (3-6 years)

### Developmental Characteristics
- **Cognitive**: Imagination explosion; begins understanding simple cause-effect; limited logical thinking
- **Emotional**: Can start naming basic emotions, but regulation is weak; frequent emotional storms
- **Social**: Beginning socialization (preschool); learning sharing and turn-taking; friendship buds
- **Autonomy**: Strong "do it myself" desire, but limited ability; easily frustrated

### Core Developmental Tasks
- Develop autonomy and initiative
- Learn basic emotion identification and naming
- Begin understanding and internalizing basic rules
- Early socialization

### Parent Role
**Emotion Coach + Boundary Setter**
- Help the child name emotions ("You're feeling really angry right now")
- Set boundaries warmly and firmly ("You can be angry, but you can't hit")
- Replace commands with choices ("Do you want to brush teeth first or pick tomorrow's clothes?")
- Use stories and games instead of lectures

### Communication Strategy
- **Emotion creature framework** fully applicable
- Use short sentences, concrete descriptions, sensory words
- No more than 2 choices
- Replace "don't" with "do" ("walk slowly" instead of "don't run")
- Stories and games are the most effective communication medium

### Age Subdivisions

#### 3-4 years
- 2-4 short lines at a time
- No abstract reasoning
- Use sound, rhythm, and movement
- At most one choice
- Use only the simplest creature images

#### 5-6 years
- 4-6 short lines
- Simple cause-and-effect is OK
- One feeling word + one body word
- One or two balanced options when calm
- Begin building "body signal → action" connections

### High-Frequency Scenarios

| Scenario | Key Points |
|----------|-----------|
| Emotional storms | Emotion creature + body signal + co-regulation action |
| Refusing brushing/bathing/sleeping | Autonomy need, not deliberate defiance; rules + choices + transition ritual |
| Preschool adjustment | Separation ritual, transitional objects, accept emotional expression |
| Screen/phone | Clear rules + buffer ritual + alternative activities |
| Refusing to share | Normal developmental stage; don't force; model turn-taking |
| Sibling rivalry | Protect the older child's sense of belonging; avoid "let your sibling have it" |

## 🌳 Rooting Stage (6-12 years)

### Developmental Characteristics
- **Cognitive**: Logical thinking developing; can understand rules and cause-effect; begins self-reflection
- **Emotional**: Emotion vocabulary expanding; starting self-regulation; still regresses under stress
- **Social**: Friendships become important; begins comparing; cares about peer evaluation
- **Autonomy**: Needs more space and decision-making power; forming self-concept

### Core Developmental Tasks
- Develop industriousness and competence
- Build self-discipline
- Learn social skills
- Rules shift from "external constraints" to "internal agreement"

### Parent Role
**Guide + Supporter**
- Shift from "doing for the child" to "doing with the child" to "watching the child do"
- Provide structure and methods, not doing it for them
- Allow mistakes; help learn from them
- Focus on process and effort, not just results

### Communication Strategy
- Ages 6-8: Emotion creatures still usable, add "translation" and reflection
- Ages 8-10: **Key transition period** — gradually move from creatures to "inner voice" to direct naming (see `emotion-creatures.md` 8-10 transition)
- Ages 10-12: Use direct emotion language and mature metaphors
- Can use simple reflective dialogue ("What do you think made you so frustrated?")
- Give more autonomy ("How would you like to arrange this?")
- Respect emerging privacy needs

### Age Subdivisions

#### 6-8 years
- Emotion creatures still usable, but begin adding "translation" ("Sparky means angry")
- Brief stories with naming and reflection
- Can include "next time we could try..."
- Stay concrete, but allow more autonomy

#### 8-10 years (Transition)
- Creatures and direct emotion language used in parallel; let the child choose
- Introduce "inner voice" framework ("Is there a voice in your head saying...?")
- Body awareness throughout
- Follow the child — don't force them to leave or keep creatures
- Detailed transition strategies → `emotion-creatures.md`

#### 10-12 years
- Do not proactively use emotion creatures (unless child requests)
- Use mature metaphors and direct emotion language
- Can discuss strategies and plans
- Value the child's opinions
- Begin "negotiating" rather than "instructing"

### High-Frequency Scenarios

| Scenario | Key Points |
|----------|-----------|
| Homework procrastination | Break tasks, pomodoro technique, environment management, find the block don't nag |
| Test anxiety | Normalize anxiety, preparation strategies, reduce results-oriented evaluation |
| School refusal | Find root cause (social? difficulty? meaning?); stay curious not anxious |
| Game/phone addiction | Negotiate rules, understand the need (social? achievement? escape?), alternative fulfillment |
| Being bullied | Listen + validate feelings; don't rush "hit back" or "tell teacher"; brainstorm together |
| No friends | Social skill practice, find comfortable social style, don't force |
| Sibling conflict | Don't be the referee; teach conflict resolution skills; see each child's needs |

## 🔥 Awakening Stage (12-15 years)

### Developmental Characteristics
- **Cognitive**: Abstract thinking developing; begins questioning authority and rules; idealism emerges
- **Emotional**: Emotional volatility increases (hormones + identity exploration); more internalized and hidden
- **Social**: Peer relationships become central; forming independent values and identity
- **Autonomy**: Strong independence needs; heightened privacy awareness; parent-child power restructuring

### Core Developmental Tasks
- Build self-identity ("Who am I")
- Develop independent thinking
- Learn to maintain self amid peer pressure
- Shift from family-centered to socially oriented

### Parent Role
**Consultant + Respecter**
- Shift completely from "manager" to "consultant" — give advice only when invited
- Listen more than lecture, be curious more than worried
- Respect privacy, maintain availability
- Hold the line on safety, but release other controls

### Communication Strategy
- **Absolutely do not use** emotion creatures or any infantilizing frameworks
- Equal, respectful tone
- Use open-ended questions, not leading ones
- Share rather than instruct ("I've been through something similar...")
- Proactively give space ("You don't have to talk about it if you don't want to — I'm here")
- Acknowledge your own limitations ("I'm not sure about this either — let's think together?")

### ⚠️ Special Attention
- Many "problem behaviors" at this age are actually normal developmental needs
- "Rebellion" is often autonomy development in action
- Forceful control typically leads to relationship breakdown or underground behavior
- **Yellow signal recognition is critical**: Persistent low mood, social withdrawal, self-harm signs require timely guidance toward professional help

### High-Frequency Scenarios

| Scenario | Key Points |
|----------|-----------|
| "Rebellion"/talking back | Normalize (this is autonomy development); don't escalate; find a moment for equal dialogue |
| Phone/internet dependency | Negotiate rather than confiscate; understand social needs; co-create rules |
| Refusing to communicate | Respect space; stay available; connect through actions not words |
| Peer pressure | Don't criticize friends; help build self-worth; practice saying "no" |
| Privacy boundaries | Respecting privacy is the foundation of trust; don't check phone/diary; unless safety red line |
| Early romance | Don't panic; don't spy; open dialogue; focus on safety and respect |
| Low mood | Recognize yellow signals; don't say "what do you have to be upset about"; guide toward professional help when needed |
| Academic pressure | Prioritize mental health over grades; reduce comparison; help find meaning |

## 🔀 Age-Scenario Cross-Guidance

The same scenario requires vastly different approaches at different ages. Key intersections:

### "Defiance" / Rule Conflict

| Age Band | Essence | Method |
|----------|---------|--------|
| 0-3 | Autonomy budding — not "defiance" | Distract, limited choices, gentle redirection |
| 3-6 | Autonomy vs. rules — initial collision | Emotion acceptance + warm firmness + choices |
| 6-12 | Desire for meaning and fairness in rules | Explain reasons + negotiate + natural consequences |
| 12-15 | Independence and power restructuring | Equal dialogue + negotiate rules + clear bottom lines |

### Screen/Phone

| Age Band | Essence | Method |
|----------|---------|--------|
| 0-3 | Mainly parent behavior guidance | Reduce parent's own phone use in front of child |
| 3-6 | Lack of alternatives + instant gratification | Clear rules + buffer ritual + rich activities |
| 6-12 | Achievement/social/escape — multiple needs | Negotiate time + understand needs + alternative fulfillment |
| 12-15 | Core social tool + identity expression | Negotiate rather than control + digital literacy + trust |

### Social Difficulties

| Age Band | Essence | Method |
|----------|---------|--------|
| 0-3 | Still in parallel play phase — no worry needed | Provide social contact opportunities |
| 3-6 | Socialization beginning; conflict is learning | Guide from the side, model social language |
| 6-12 | Friendship importance rising; exclusion really hurts | Listen + validate + social skill practice |
| 12-15 | Peer relationships are central; parent influence decreases | Don't criticize friends; be the safe harbor; practice boundaries |

## Usage Rules

1. Identify the age band first for every request, then select corresponding communication strategies
2. If the user doesn't state age, infer from description (e.g., "preschool" → 3-6, "middle school" → 12-15)
3. If unable to infer, briefly ask "How old is the child roughly?"
4. For boundary ages (3, 6, 12), judge flexibly based on the specific description — don't cut mechanically
5. Multiple children in the same family may be at different stages — consider each separately
